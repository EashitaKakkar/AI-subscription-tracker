# 👥 USER_INTERVIEWS.md

These interviews were conducted with students, tech enthusiasts, working professionals and early-career developers who actively use or explore AI tools for productivity, learning, and development workflows.

The goal was to understand:

- How users currently manage AI subscriptions
- Whether cost optimization is a real pain point
- User reactions to email-gated “free” tools
- How trust impacts onboarding behavior

---

# 1. Osman İpek — SDE

**Role:** Working Professional 
**Stage:** Individual user experimenting with AI tools for education and programming

## Key Quotes

> “I currently pay for ChatGPT Plus because I use it almost every day for programming and debugging.”

> “The difference between the free and paid version is huge.”

> “If a tool showed me I was wasting money, I would probably switch immediately.”

> “I hate when ‘free’ tools ask for email verification before showing anything useful.”

## Most Surprising Insight

The most surprising thing Osman mentioned was that even a *few seconds* of forced signup friction made him abandon tools entirely, despite already being comfortable paying for AI products.

He specifically compared the experience to trying Claude for the first time and feeling frustrated before even testing the product.

## What It Changed in SpendLens

This interview directly changed the onboarding flow.

Originally, SpendLens required email capture before showing audit results. After this conversation, I redesigned the flow so users see immediate value first, while email capture happens later as part of the “shareable audit” experience.

It also reinforced the importance of:

- Fast loading
- Minimal friction
- Instant audit visibility

for trust-building.

# 2.  Saujanya + Elfen — Students Exploring AI Productivity

**Role:** Students / Casual AI Users  
**Stage:** Free-tier AI users considering premium upgrades

## Key Quotes

> “I mostly use free versions of AI tools unless I really need something specific.”

> “If I paid for something, I’d want it to actually save me time.”

> “I would switch immediately if I knew I was wasting $50.”

> “Sometimes login feels annoying if I only want to use a tool once.”

## Most Surprising Insight

Both users viewed AI subscriptions less like software and more like utilities.

They didn’t care about “brand loyalty” to tools like ChatGPT or Claude — they only cared whether the subscription actively improved productivity enough to justify recurring cost.

This contradicted my earlier assumption that users become emotionally attached to AI ecosystems.

## What It Changed in SpendLens

This changed how I framed the audit recommendations.

Instead of emphasizing:
- “best AI tools”

the product shifted toward:
- “eliminating unnecessary overlap”
- “maximizing value per subscription”
- “team efficiency”

It also influenced the design of the recommendation cards, making them more savings-focused and less feature-focused.

# 3.  FK — AI & Software Engineering Student

**Role:** Software Engineering Student and Intern at Fontys  
**Stage:** Heavy AI-assisted development workflow

## Key Quotes

> “Without ChatGPT Plus, my study process would be much slower.”

> “Advanced models understand context way better.”

> “I would still quickly check if I needed the service before cancelling.”

> “When something says ‘free,’ I expect value before giving personal information.”

## Most Surprising Insight

FK introduced an important nuance that other interviewees didn’t mention:

Even when users *know* they are wasting money, they still hesitate to cancel immediately because they fear losing future convenience.

This revealed that subscription waste is often emotional, not purely financial.

## What It Changed in SpendLens

This interview influenced the tone of the executive summaries generated by the AI.

Instead of aggressively telling users to “cancel” subscriptions, the summaries now focus on:

- Consolidation
- Optimization
- Tier restructuring
- Smarter allocation

This made the product feel more like a strategic financial advisor rather than a subscription-cleanup app.

It also reinforced the importance of maintaining a professional tone throughout the platform.

---

# Overall Takeaways

Across all interviews, several consistent patterns emerged:

## Common Pain Points

- Subscription overlap is difficult to track manually
- Users dislike email-gated onboarding
- Most people care more about *time saved* than tool branding
- Users want actionable insights, not generic recommendations

## Design Changes Triggered

These conversations directly influenced:

- The delayed email capture flow
- The professional/no-fluff AI tone
- The savings-first dashboard structure
- The simplified recommendation system
- The focus on actionable optimization instead of “tool discovery”

The interviews validated that AI subscription management is not just a budgeting problem — it is fundamentally a workflow and trust problem.
